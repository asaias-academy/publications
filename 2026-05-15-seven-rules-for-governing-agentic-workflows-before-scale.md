# Seven Rules for Governing Agentic Workflows Before Scale

Status: approved public publication v1  
Release date: 2026-05-15  
Format: practical publication

## Purpose

This publication is written for founders, managers and institutional operators who are under pressure to deploy AI agents quickly. Its purpose is to slow the conversation down just enough to keep scale governable.

## The seven rules

### 1. Define the workflow before choosing the model

If the workflow boundary is unclear, model choice becomes theater. Start by defining:

- what task is being delegated;
- what counts as completion;
- what actions are allowed;
- what requires escalation.

### 2. Keep tools and interfaces explicit

An agent is not only a model. It is a model plus tools, context and instructions. Before scale, organizations should know:

- what systems are connected;
- what the agent can read;
- what the agent can change;
- what remains out of scope.

### 3. Design human approval before autonomy expands

Human approval should not be added later as a ceremonial checkbox. It should be designed early:

- who can authorize execution;
- who can pause or refuse;
- which outputs require final sign-off;
- what happens when the result is uncertain.

### 4. Treat traces and evals as core infrastructure

If an organization cannot inspect runs, compare outcomes or review failure patterns, it does not yet have a mature AI workflow. Traces, evaluation loops and review discipline are trust infrastructure.

### 5. Separate protocol choice from vendor hype

Interoperability matters because systems must connect safely and legibly across tools and organizations. Protocol discussion should therefore be framed around:

- reliability;
- boundaries;
- auditability;
- long-term governability.

### 6. Publish responsibility contours internally

Every meaningful workflow should have visible responsibility contours:

- who framed it;
- who authorized it;
- who observed it;
- who could intervene;
- who handled consequences afterward.

### 7. Scale only after one audited pilot route

Organizations should resist multiplying surfaces too early. One pilot route with visible controls, evidence and approved corrections is worth more than a broad but opaque rollout.
